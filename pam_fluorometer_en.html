🌿 PAM Fluorometer — Virtual Laboratory

Study of photosynthetic activity in Arabidopsis thaliana leaves using pulse amplitude modulated (PAM) fluorometry. Goal: determine PSII quantum yield, electron transport rate, and non-photochemical quenching under different light conditions and stress treatments.

Why this experiment? Chlorophyll absorbs light and can use that energy via three pathways: photochemistry (productive photosynthesis), heat dissipation (protection), and fluorescence (emission ~1–2%). The PAM fluorometer measures this emission in real time. By analysing fluorescence changes we can calculate how much energy drives photosynthesis (ETR), how efficient the reaction centres are (Fv/Fm), and how the plant protects itself from excess light (NPQ) — without destroying the leaf.

Parameter Reference

F₀
Minimal fluorescence — signal when the weak measuring light is turned on. All PSII reaction centres are open (ready to work). Higher F₀ under stress signals more damage.
Fm
Maximum fluorescence — reached after a saturating flash that closes all reaction centres simultaneously. Measured on a dark-adapted leaf.
Fv/Fm
Maximum quantum yield of PSII — the primary indicator of photosynthetic health. Normal ≈ 0.83. Decrease signals stress or damage.
Fv/Fm = (Fm − F₀) / Fm
Fm′
Maximum fluorescence under actinic light. Always lower than Fm — because some energy is dissipated as heat via NPQ. Measured with repeated saturating flashes.
NPQ
Non-photochemical quenching — active protection from excess light. The plant dissipates surplus energy as heat via the xanthophyll cycle. The brighter the light, the higher the NPQ.
NPQ = Fm/Fm′ − 1
qP
Photochemical quenching — the fraction of open reaction centres under actinic light. qP = 1: all RCs open; qP = 0: all closed (light-saturated).
qP = (Fm′ − Fs) / (Fm′ − F₀′)
ETR
Linear electron transport rate — actual photosynthetic output. Shows how many electrons per second pass through PSII per m² of leaf.
ETR = (Fq′/Fm′) × PAR × 0.5 × 0.84
Fs
Steady-state fluorescence — signal level during steady photosynthesis (actinic light on, no flash). Used to calculate all "working" parameters.
PAR
Photosynthetically active radiation — actinic light intensity (400–700 nm). Units: µmol photons/m²·s. Full midday sun ≈ 1800–2000.

Experimental Protocol — what to do and why

1
Dark adaptation (30 s in simulation = 30 min in reality) — all reaction centres oxidise and "open", NPQ relaxes. Without this, F₀ and Fm will be underestimated.
2
Turn on measuring light — very weak (<0.1 µmol/m²·s), does not drive photosynthesis, only registers F₀.
3
Saturating flash → Fm — a flash >6000 µmol/m²·s for 0.8 s closes ALL RCs → Fm peak. Then calculate Fv/Fm.
4
Actinic light — working illumination drives photosynthesis. The OJIP transient is visible (Fs rises sharply), then slowly drops as NPQ builds and RCs reopen.
5
Repeat flashes → Fm′ — every 1–2 min apply a flash and record Fm′. Calculate NPQ, qP, ETR. Watch NPQ rise and Fm′ drop.

Electron transport rate = actual photosynthetic output. High at start of actinic phase (NPQ=0, qP≈1) — this is normal! Stabilises lower as NPQ builds.
Why is ETR high at the start of actinic phase? In the first minutes, NPQ has not yet built up (xanthophyll cycle is slow), all reaction centres are open (qP≈1) — Fq′/Fm′ ≈ Fv/Fm. Therefore electron transport is maximally efficient. As NPQ accumulates and RCs partially close, ETR stabilises at a lower level.
