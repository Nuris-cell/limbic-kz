TASK 1

Basic parameters of a dark-adapted leaf

▼
Measurements from a dark-adapted leaf: F₀ = 120, Fm = 680 (rel. units)
a Calculate Fv and Fv/Fm
Use the formulas: Fv = Fm − F₀ and Fv/Fm = Fv / Fm. Does the value match the normal range (~0.83)?
Fv = rel. units
Fv/Fm =
Fv = Fm − F₀ = 680 − 120 = 560
Fv/Fm = 560 / 680 = 0.824 — close to the normal value 0.83 ✓

Fv/Fm = maximum quantum yield of PSII photochemistry, measured when all reaction centres are open (PQ pool oxidised). It reflects the "potential efficiency" of the photosynthetic apparatus.
b Prove algebraically: Fv/Fm = 1 − F₀/Fm
Derive from the definition. Then answer: if F₀ rises while Fm stays constant — does Fv/Fm increase or decrease?
Fv/Fm when F₀=200, Fm=680:
Proof:
Fv/Fm = (Fm−F₀)/Fm = Fm/Fm − F₀/Fm = 1 − F₀/Fm

With F₀ = 200, Fm = 680:
Fv/Fm = 1 − 200/680 = 1 − 0.294 = 0.706

↑F₀ → ↓Fv/Fm. The biological reason: D1 subunit degradation during photoinhibition. Damaged PSII reaction centres "waste" excitation as fluorescence instead of photochemistry, raising F₀.
c ★ Why does Fv/Fm never reach 1?
Hint: consider the competing deactivation pathways of excited chlorophyll.
Excited chlorophyll (Chl*) has three competing deactivation pathways:

  • Photochemistry (kp) — energy transfer to the reaction centre → electron transfer
  • Fluorescence (kf) — photon emission
  • Thermal dissipation (kd) — radiationless transition

Fv/Fm = kp / (kp + kf + kd). Since kf and kd are always > 0, the denominator exceeds the numerator, so Fv/Fm < 1.

Even under "ideal" conditions F₀ > 0, because chlorophyll always fluoresces and dissipates some heat — this is a physical constraint.

b Why does Fs rise sharply in the first 2 minutes?
When actinic light is switched on, PSII absorbs light immediately, but ferredoxin–NADP⁺-reductase and the Calvin cycle have not yet ramped up. The PQ pool is rapidly reduced → PSII reaction centres "close" (Q_A reduced) → electrons cannot leave → Fs rises (I-peak of OJIP).

Then over 4–10 min the Calvin cycle activates, the PQ pool "opens", and simultaneously NPQ increases → Fs declines to the steady state (S-level).

This is the Kautsky curve (OJIP transient). The high Fs at t=2 indicates temporarily "closed" (reduced) PSII reaction centres.
c Calculate ETR at t = 2 min and t = 10 min
ETR = Fq'/Fm' × PAR × 0.5 × 0.84
ETR (t = 2 min) = µmol e⁻/m²·s
ETR (t = 10 min) = µmol e⁻/m²·s
t = 2 min: Fq'/Fm' = 0.456
ETR = 0.456 × 300 × 0.5 × 0.84 = 57.5

t = 10 min: Fq'/Fm' = 0.512
ETR = 0.512 × 300 × 0.5 × 0.84 = 64.5

ETR is lower at t=2 — reaction centres are "closed" (qP=0.56), restricting electron flow. By t=10 the system has reached steady state (qP=0.63), more centres are open, so throughput is higher.

b Drought paradox: Fv/Fm is only moderately reduced, yet NPQ is very low (0.4)
Why does the plant fail to activate photoprotection when stomata are closed?
The key is the thylakoid ΔpH.

During drought stomata close → CO₂ cannot enter → Calvin cycle slows → NADPH/NADP⁺ ratio rises → electron transport slows → protons are not actively pumped into the thylakoid lumen → ΔpH remains low → lumenal pH does not drop → VDE (violaxanthin de-epoxidase) is not activated → zeaxanthin is not formed → NPQ stays low.

The paradox: precisely when light is most dangerous (low CO₂), the ΔpH-dependent NPQ cannot engage fully.
c Photoinhibition: F₀ rose strongly, Fm dropped. Explain at the molecular level.
Excess light leads to accumulation of singlet oxygen (¹O₂) and other ROS, which attack the D1 subunit of the PSII reaction centre.

  • ↑F₀ — damaged centres cannot perform photochemistry (Q_A is blocked), all absorbed energy becomes fluorescence → baseline rises
  • ↓Fm — fewer functional PSII units can reach Fm during a saturating flash

Reversibility: Partially yes. D1 is the most rapidly turned-over chloroplast protein. At lower light intensities, D1 is re-synthesised within hours (FtsH protease removes the damaged D1; ribosomes insert a new one). However, severe photoinhibition leads to incomplete recovery.
d N deficiency: Fm is strongly reduced with only moderate F₀ rise
Under nitrogen deficiency the plant primarily dismantles light-harvesting complexes (LHCII) — these are the most protein-rich structures in the chloroplast and serve as an N reserve.

  • ↓Fm — fewer antenna chlorophylls → lower total fluorescence at saturation
  • Moderate F₀ — the reaction centre baseline is preserved (D1 intact), but with fewer antennae F₀ rises only slightly

The diagnostic signature of N deficiency is a drop in Fm with relatively intact F₀ and moderate Fv/Fm (~0.67).

a At what PAR does the NPQ difference become physiologically significant?
At PAR = 100: ΔNPQ = 0.3 − 0.1 = 0.2 — negligible (low light).
At PAR = 300: ΔNPQ = 1.8 − 0.4 = 1.4 — already significant. ETR starts to diverge (52 vs 48).
At PAR ≥ 600: ΔNPQ = 2.5, ΔETR = 10 — clear difference in photoprotection.

The difference becomes physiologically significant at PAR ≥ 300 µmol/m²·s — the level of moderate daylight at which the xanthophyll cycle is critically important.
b ETR of npq1 drops at high PAR — explain the mechanism
npq1 has no zeaxanthin → no efficient dissipation of excess excitation.

Under excess light, triplet chlorophyll (³Chl*) accumulates in PSII → reacts with O₂ → singlet oxygen (¹O₂) → attacks the D1 subunit.

D1 degradation → PSII photoinhibition → fewer functional reaction centres → ↓ETR.

In WT, zeaxanthin safely quenches ³Chl* (via conformational changes in LHC), D1 is preserved → ETR stays stable even at 1800.
c How many more electrons flow into alternative reactions in npq1 at PAR = 1800?
ETR WT = 62, ETR npq1 = 31. Calculate the difference and the percentage of "excess" electrons in npq1.
ΔETR = µmol e⁻/m²·s
% more in npq1 = %
ΔETR = 62 − 31 = 31 µmol e⁻/m²·s

% = 31/62 × 100 = 50%

So npq1 at PAR=1800 channels only half as many electrons through productive transport. The "excess" electrons in npq1 go to:
  • Mehler reaction (photoreduction of O₂): e⁻ + O₂ → O₂•⁻ (superoxide)
  • Photorespiration: accumulating O₂ causes Rubisco to oxygenate RuBP, wasting electrons
d ★ Residual NPQ ~1.0 in npq1: two zeaxanthin-independent mechanisms
How can they be distinguished experimentally using a PAM fluorometer?
Residual NPQ without zeaxanthin includes:

1. qE (ΔpH-dependent, PsbS-dependent)
Conformational quenching via PsbS at low lumenal pH — even without zeaxanthin, PsbS is partially activated. Rapidly reversible in darkness (seconds–minutes).

2. qI (photoinhibitory quenching)
Damaged D1-containing PSII centres themselves quench fluorescence. Very slowly reversible (hours).

How to distinguish with the PAM fluorometer:
Relaxation kinetics protocol:
  • Switch off actinic light → monitor recovery of Fm' towards Fm
  • qE relaxes quickly: t½ ~ 1–2 min in the dark
  • qI persists for hours: after 30 min of darkness Fm is still below the initial value
Additionally: far-red light (FR) lowers ΔpH → removes qE but not qI.
